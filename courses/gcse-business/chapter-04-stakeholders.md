# Chapter 4: Stakeholders

## Learning Objectives
- Define what a stakeholder is
- Identify internal and external stakeholders
- Understand different stakeholder interests
- Analyze conflicts between stakeholders
- Evaluate how businesses manage stakeholder relationships

## 4.1 What are Stakeholders?

### Definition

A **stakeholder** is any individual or group that has an interest in or is affected by the activities of a business.

### Why Stakeholders Matter

- Influence business decisions
- Affect business success
- Have competing interests
- Can help or hinder operations
- Require management and communication

## 4.2 Types of Stakeholders

### Internal Stakeholders

**Definition**: Groups within the organization

**Examples**:
- Owners/Shareholders
- Managers
- Employees

**Characteristics**:
- Direct involvement in business
- Immediate impact on operations
- Daily interaction
- Strong influence on decisions

### External Stakeholders

**Definition**: Groups outside the organization

**Examples**:
- Customers
- Suppliers
- Community
- Government
- Banks/Creditors
- Competitors

**Characteristics**:
- Indirect involvement
- External influence
- Varied interests
- Less control over business

## 4.3 Internal Stakeholders

### 1. Owners/Shareholders

**Who they are**:
- Sole traders
- Partners
- Shareholders in Ltd/PLC companies

**Their interests**:
✓ High profits and dividends  
✓ Business growth and success  
✓ Return on investment  
✓ Increasing share value  
✓ Long-term sustainability

**How they influence business**:
- Make strategic decisions (especially in small businesses)
- Vote at AGMs (in companies)
- Hire/fire directors
- Approve major changes
- Provide capital

**Potential conflicts**:
- **vs Employees**: Want to minimize wages to maximize profit
- **vs Customers**: May prioritize profit over quality
- **vs Community**: May ignore environmental concerns for profit

**Example**: Amazon shareholders want maximum profit, which may conflict with employee demands for better working conditions.

### 2. Managers

**Who they are**:
- Directors
- Department heads
- Team leaders
- Supervisors

**Their interests**:
✓ Job security  
✓ Salary and bonuses  
✓ Career progression  
✓ Authority and respect  
✓ Business success  
✓ Resources to do job effectively

**How they influence business**:
- Make operational decisions
- Implement strategy
- Manage employees
- Control budgets
- Report to owners

**Potential conflicts**:
- **vs Owners**: Want investment in department vs profit distribution
- **vs Employees**: Must balance motivation with cost control
- **vs Customers**: Efficiency targets may reduce service quality

**Example**: Managers at retail stores may want more staff for better service, but owners want to minimize wage costs.

### 3. Employees

**Who they are**:
- Full-time and part-time staff
- Temporary workers
- Contractors

**Their interests**:
✓ Job security  
✓ Fair wages and benefits  
✓ Safe working conditions  
✓ Training and development  
✓ Work-life balance  
✓ Job satisfaction  
✓ Recognition and respect

**How they influence business**:
- Productivity levels affect output
- Can strike or work-to-rule
- Word-of-mouth recruitment
- Customer service quality
- Innovation and ideas

**Potential conflicts**:
- **vs Owners**: Want higher wages vs owners want lower costs
- **vs Customers**: May want shorter hours vs customer convenience
- **vs Managers**: May resist change or increased workload

**Example**: Amazon warehouse workers demanded better pay and conditions, conflicting with company's profit goals.

## 4.4 External Stakeholders

### 1. Customers

**Who they are**:
- Individual consumers
- Business clients
- Wholesalers/Retailers (for manufacturers)

**Their interests**:
✓ Quality products/services  
✓ Low prices  
✓ Good customer service  
✓ Value for money  
✓ Ethical business practices  
✓ Product safety  
✓ Convenience

**How they influence business**:
- Choose whether to buy products
- Online reviews and recommendations
- Complaints and feedback
- Loyalty vs switching to competitors
- Social media influence

**Potential conflicts**:
- **vs Owners**: Want low prices vs business wants high profit margins
- **vs Suppliers**: Demand for cheap products pressures supplier prices
- **vs Employees**: Self-service checkouts vs jobs

**Example**: Customers want fast fashion at low prices, but this may conflict with ethical sourcing from suppliers.

### 2. Suppliers

**Who they are**:
- Raw material providers
- Component manufacturers
- Service providers
- Wholesalers

**Their interests**:
✓ Regular, large orders  
✓ Fair payment terms  
✓ On-time payment  
✓ Long-term contracts  
✓ Good business relationship  
✓ Fair prices for their goods

**How they influence business**:
- Quality of supplies affects product quality
- Delivery reliability impacts production
- Credit terms affect cash flow
- Can refuse to supply
- Pricing affects costs

**Potential conflicts**:
- **vs Business**: Want high prices vs business wants low costs
- **vs Customers**: May compromise quality to meet business cost pressures
- **vs Owners**: Payment terms (suppliers want quick payment, businesses want extended credit)

**Example**: Supermarkets pressure suppliers for lower prices, squeezing their profit margins.

### 3. Local Community

**Who they are**:
- Local residents
- Community groups
- Environmental organizations
- Local businesses

**Their interests**:
✓ Employment opportunities  
✓ Minimal environmental impact  
✓ No noise/traffic pollution  
✓ Support for local events  
✓ Business rate contributions  
✓ Safe and ethical operations

**How they influence business**:
- Protests and campaigns
- Planning permission objections
- Boycotts
- Social media pressure
- Support or hostility

**Potential conflicts**:
- **vs Owners**: Expansion may cause pollution but increases profits
- **vs Employees**: Factory closure helps business but causes local unemployment
- **vs Customers**: Ethical concerns (e.g., Amazon warehouse conditions) vs convenient service

**Example**: Local residents may oppose new factory due to traffic and pollution, even though it creates jobs.

### 4. Government

**Who they are**:
- Local councils
- National government
- Regulatory bodies (e.g., HSE, Competition Authority)
- Tax authorities (HMRC)

**Their interests**:
✓ Tax revenue  
✓ Legal compliance  
✓ Employment creation  
✓ Economic growth  
✓ Fair competition  
✓ Consumer protection  
✓ Environmental protection

**How they influence business**:
- Laws and regulations
- Tax rates and policies
- Grants and subsidies
- Planning permissions
- Fines and penalties
- Public contracts

**Potential conflicts**:
- **vs Owners**: Taxes reduce profit; regulations increase costs
- **vs Employees**: Minimum wage increases costs for business
- **vs Customers**: Regulations (e.g., safety standards) may increase prices

**Example**: Government introduces higher minimum wage (helps employees) but increases business costs (reduces profit).

### 5. Banks and Creditors

**Who they are**:
- Banks providing loans/overdrafts
- Investors
- Bondholders
- Trade creditors

**Their interests**:
✓ Loan repayment on time  
✓ Interest payments  
✓ Business solvency  
✓ Low-risk investments  
✓ Profitable business  
✓ Good credit rating

**How they influence business**:
- Approve or deny loans
- Set interest rates
- Demand collateral
- Can force bankruptcy if not repaid
- Influence business decisions through loan conditions

**Potential conflicts**:
- **vs Owners**: Want guaranteed returns vs risky growth strategies
- **vs Employees**: May force redundancies to ensure loan repayment
- **vs Customers**: Pressure to increase prices to improve profitability

**Example**: Banks may refuse loans for expansion, limiting business growth ambitions.

### 6. Competitors

**Who they are**:
- Direct competitors (same products/markets)
- Indirect competitors (substitute products)
- Potential new entrants

**Their interests**:
✓ Gain market share  
✓ Win customers  
✓ Increase profits  
✓ Industry standards  
✓ Fair competition

**How they influence business**:
- Pricing strategies affect market prices
- Innovation forces improvement
- Marketing campaigns compete for attention
- May collaborate on industry issues
- Benchmark performance

**Potential conflicts**:
- **vs Business**: Compete for same customers
- **vs Customers**: Price wars may benefit customers
- **vs Employees**: Poaching skilled staff

**Example**: Budget airlines compete aggressively on price, forcing rivals to cut costs or differentiate.

### 7. Trade Unions

**Who they are**:
- Organizations representing workers
- Examples: Unite, UNISON, GMB

**Their interests**:
✓ Better wages and conditions for members  
✓ Job security  
✓ Workplace safety  
✓ Fair treatment  
✓ Negotiating power

**How they influence business**:
- Collective bargaining
- Strike action
- Work-to-rule
- Publicity campaigns
- Legal challenges

**Potential conflicts**:
- **vs Owners**: Wage demands reduce profit
- **vs Customers**: Strikes disrupt service
- **vs Managers**: Challenge management decisions

**Example**: Rail union strikes disrupt services, affecting customers and business revenue, to pressure for better pay.

## 4.5 Stakeholder Mapping

### Power-Interest Matrix

Stakeholders can be categorized by their power and interest levels:

```
High Power, High Interest → KEY PLAYERS (manage closely)
- Major shareholders
- Senior managers
- Key customers

High Power, Low Interest → KEEP SATISFIED
- Government regulators
- Large creditors

Low Power, High Interest → KEEP INFORMED
- Employee groups
- Community groups
- Consumer associations

Low Power, Low Interest → MONITOR (minimum effort)
- Distant suppliers
- General public
```

## 4.6 Managing Stakeholder Relationships

### Communication Methods

**Internal**:
- Team meetings
- Emails and intranet
- Performance reviews
- Staff surveys
- Annual reports

**External**:
- Press releases
- Social media
- Customer service
- AGMs (for shareholders)
- Community events

### Strategies for Managing Conflicts

#### 1. Prioritization
Decide which stakeholders are most important

**Example**: Airlines prioritize safety (regulators) over passenger comfort during crises.

#### 2. Consultation
Involve stakeholders in decisions

**Example**: Tesco consults local communities before opening new stores.

#### 3. Compromise
Find middle-ground solutions

**Example**: Business offers moderate wage increase (not as high as unions want, but higher than owners prefer).

#### 4. Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)
Balance profit with social/environmental concerns

**Example**: Starbucks sources ethically and supports communities while maintaining profitability.

#### 5. Transparency
Open communication about decisions and performance

**Example**: Public companies publish detailed annual reports for shareholders.

## 4.7 Stakeholder Conflict Examples

### Case 1: Factory Closure

**Scenario**: Company wants to close UK factory and move production to China to reduce costs.

**Stakeholder impacts**:
- ✅ **Shareholders**: Increased profit from lower costs
- ❌ **Employees**: Job losses
- ❌ **Community**: Economic decline, unemployment
- ❌ **Government**: Lost tax revenue
- ✅ **Customers**: Potentially lower prices
- ❌ **Suppliers**: Loss of business

### Case 2: Price Increase

**Scenario**: Business increases prices by 20% to improve profit margins.

**Stakeholder impacts**:
- ✅ **Owners**: Higher profit
- ❌ **Customers**: Pay more
- ⚠️ **Employees**: May benefit from profit-sharing, but risk job security if sales fall
- ⚠️ **Competitors**: May gain customers if prices are too high

### Case 3: Environmental Investment

**Scenario**: Company invests £10 million in renewable energy and waste reduction.

**Stakeholder impacts**:
- ❌ **Shareholders**: Lower short-term dividends
- ✅ **Community**: Reduced pollution
- ✅ **Government**: Meets environmental targets
- ✅ **Employees**: Pride in ethical employer
- ✅ **Customers**: Prefer sustainable brand
- ✅ **Long-term owners**: Sustainable business model

## Key Terminology

- **Stakeholder**: Individual or group with interest in business
- **Internal Stakeholder**: Group within organization (owners, managers, employees)
- **External Stakeholder**: Group outside organization (customers, suppliers, community)
- **Conflict of Interest**: Disagreement between stakeholder aims
- **Power**: Ability to influence business decisions
- **Interest**: Level of concern about business activities
- **CSR (Corporate Social Responsibility)**: Business commitment to ethical and sustainable practices
- **Trade Union**: Organization representing workers' interests
- **Dividend**: Payment to shareholders from profits
- **Collective Bargaining**: Negotiation between unions and employers

## Exam-Style Questions

### Short Answer Questions

1. Define what is meant by a 'stakeholder'. (2 marks)

2. State two interests of employees in a business. (2 marks)

3. Identify one internal and one external stakeholder. (2 marks)

### Extended Questions

4. Explain three ways customers can influence a business. (6 marks)

5. Analyze two conflicts that might exist between different stakeholders in a business. (6 marks)

6. Discuss the view that shareholders are always the most important stakeholder group. (12 marks)

### Case Study Question

**Case Study: SportsCo Ltd**

SportsCo manufactures sports equipment in the UK. The business is considering two options:

**Option A**: Increase prices by 15% to invest in better quality materials and pay employees higher wages.

**Option B**: Maintain current prices but relocate production to India where labor costs are 60% lower.

Current situation:
- 200 UK employees
- Located in town with 20% unemployment
- Shareholders receive 5% dividend annually
- Quality complaints have increased by 30%

**Questions**:

a) State two internal stakeholders in SportsCo. (2 marks)

b) Identify two external stakeholders that would be affected by Option B. (2 marks)

c) Explain how the interests of employees differ from those of shareholders. (4 marks)

d) Analyze the impact of Option A on two different stakeholder groups. (6 marks)

e) Evaluate which option SportsCo should choose. Consider the impact on different stakeholders in your answer. (12 marks)

✅ Internal: owners, managers, employees (inside business)  
✅ External: customers, suppliers, community, government, banks, competitors (outside business)  
✅ Each group has different interests and objectives  
✅ Conflicts arise when interests don't align  
✅ Owners want profit; employees want wages; customers want low prices  
✅ Community wants jobs and clean environment; government wants taxes  
✅ Suppliers want regular orders; competitors want market share  
✅ Managing stakeholders requires prioritization, communication, and compromise  
✅ CSR helps balance profit with social and environmental concerns
