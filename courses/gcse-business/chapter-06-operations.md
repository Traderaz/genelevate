# Chapter 6: Operations Management

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the role of operations management
- Explain production methods and processes
- Analyze quality management approaches
- Evaluate inventory management techniques
- Understand supply chains and logistics
- Examine customer service and satisfaction

## 6.1 What is Operations Management?

### Definition

**Operations management** is the process of managing resources to produce and deliver goods and services efficiently.

### Key Objectives

✓ Produce quality products/services  
✓ Meet customer demand  
✓ Minimize costs  
✓ Operate efficiently  
✓ Ensure timely delivery  
✓ Maintain safety standards

### Operations Manager Responsibilities

- Plan production schedules
- Manage inventory levels
- Ensure quality standards
- Coordinate supply chain
- Monitor efficiency
- Implement improvements
- Manage capacity

## 6.2 Production Methods

### 1. Job Production

**Definition**: Making one-off, unique products to customer specifications

**Examples**:
- Custom furniture
- Wedding cakes
- Bespoke tailoring
- House building
- Ship construction

**Characteristics**:
- Highly skilled workers
- Specialized equipment
- High quality
- Long production time
- Each product different

**Advantages**:
✓ Meets exact customer needs  
✓ High quality craftsmanship  
✓ Flexible - can adapt design  
✓ Skilled workers motivated  
✓ Premium pricing possible

**Disadvantages**:
❌ Slow production  
❌ High labor costs  
❌ Expensive per unit  
❌ Requires highly skilled workers  
❌ Difficult to achieve economies of scale

**Best for**: Custom, one-off products with high value

### 2. Batch Production

**Definition**: Making groups (batches) of identical products before switching to different batch

**Examples**:
- Bakery products (batch of croissants, then batch of bread)
- Clothing (batch of blue shirts, then red shirts)
- Pharmaceuticals
- Textbooks

**Characteristics**:
- Production in stages/batches
- Equipment reset between batches
- Some variety possible
- Semi-skilled workers

**Advantages**:
✓ Lower unit costs than job production  
✓ Some variety and flexibility  
✓ Can respond to different demands  
✓ More efficient than job production  
✓ Spreads fixed costs

**Disadvantages**:
❌ Downtime between batches  
❌ Unsold stock may be wasted  
❌ Less flexible than job production  
❌ More expensive than flow production  
❌ Work can be repetitive

**Best for**: Products with moderate variety and medium volume

### 3. Flow Production (Mass Production)

**Definition**: Continuous production of standardized products on assembly line

**Examples**:
- Cars
- Electronics
- Soft drinks
- Chocolate bars
- Newspapers

**Characteristics**:
- Continuous process
- High volume
- Standardized products
- Automated machinery
- Division of labor

**Advantages**:
✓ Very low unit costs  
✓ High output/productivity  
✓ Consistent quality  
✓ Economies of scale  
✓ 24/7 operation possible

**Disadvantages**:
❌ Very high setup costs  
❌ Inflexible - difficult to change  
❌ Breakdowns stop entire line  
❌ Repetitive work demotivates  
❌ High stock requirements

**Best for**: Mass-market standardized products

### 4. Cell Production

**Definition**: Production divided into self-contained units (cells), each completing whole section

**Example**: Car manufacturing - one cell builds entire dashboard, another cell does paintwork

**Characteristics**:
- Teams work in cells
- Complete sections of product
- Multi-skilled workers
- Teamwork important

**Advantages**:
✓ Motivating for workers (variety)  
✓ Better quality (team responsibility)  
✓ More flexible than flow production  
✓ Faster problem-solving  
✓ Workers develop multiple skills

**Disadvantages**:
❌ Requires extensive training  
❌ May need duplicate equipment  
❌ Can be costly to set up  
❌ Productivity may be lower than flow

**Best for**: Complex products requiring teamwork and quality

## 6.3 Technology in Production

### Automation

**Definition**: Using machines and technology to perform tasks without human intervention

**Examples**:
- Robotic assembly lines
- Automated checkouts
- Computer-controlled machinery
- Automated warehouses

**Advantages**:
✓ Higher productivity  
✓ Consistent quality  
✓ Lower long-term costs  
✓ 24/7 operation  
✓ Safer (dangerous tasks)  
✓ Precision and accuracy

**Disadvantages**:
❌ High initial costs  
❌ Job losses  
❌ Expensive maintenance  
❌ Breakdowns costly  
❌ Lack of flexibility  
❌ Technology becomes obsolete

### Computer-Aided Design (CAD)

**Definition**: Using computer software to design products

**Benefits**:
- Visualize in 3D
- Easy modifications
- Test virtually
- Share designs electronically
- Reduce prototyping costs

**Examples**: AutoCAD, SolidWorks, SketchUp

### Computer-Aided Manufacture (CAM)

**Definition**: Using computers to control manufacturing equipment

**Benefits**:
- Precision production
- Consistent quality
- Integration with CAD
- Reduced waste
- Faster production

**Examples**: CNC machines, 3D printers, laser cutters

## 6.4 Quality

### What is Quality?

**Quality** = Meeting or exceeding customer expectations consistently

### Why Quality Matters

**Benefits of High Quality**:
✓ Customer satisfaction and loyalty  
✓ Positive reputation  
✓ Premium pricing  
✓ Competitive advantage  
✓ Reduced waste and costs  
✓ Fewer returns and complaints

**Costs of Poor Quality**:
❌ Lost customers  
❌ Damaged reputation  
❌ Returns and refunds  
❌ Warranty claims  
❌ Waste and rework  
❌ Legal issues (safety)

### Quality Control vs Quality Assurance

#### Quality Control

**Definition**: Checking products at end of production to find defects

**Process**:
1. Production continues
2. Sample products inspected
3. Defects identified
4. Faulty products rejected or reworked

**Advantages**:
✓ Prevents faulty products reaching customers  
✓ Clear standards  
✓ Specialist inspectors

**Disadvantages**:
❌ Reactive (finds faults after production)  
❌ Waste (faulty products already made)  
❌ Inspectors expensive  
❌ Demotivating for workers

#### Quality Assurance

**Definition**: Building quality into every stage of production process

**Approach**:
- Prevention rather than detection
- All employees responsible
- Focus on processes, not just products
- Continuous improvement

**Advantages**:
✓ Prevents defects occurring  
✓ Reduces waste  
✓ Motivates workers (ownership)  
✓ Long-term cost savings

**Disadvantages**:
❌ Requires cultural change  
❌ Training costs  
❌ Time to implement  
❌ Needs employee buy-in

### Total Quality Management (TQM)

**Definition**: Continuous improvement approach involving all employees

**Key Principles**:
1. Customer focus
2. Continuous improvement (Kaizen)
3. Employee involvement
4. Prevention not detection
5. Zero defects goal

**Requirements**:
- Quality culture
- Employee empowerment
- Training
- Teamwork
- Management commitment

**Benefits**:
✓ Higher quality  
✓ Lower costs (less waste)  
✓ Motivated workforce  
✓ Customer satisfaction  
✓ Competitive advantage

**Challenges**:
❌ Long implementation time  
❌ Requires culture change  
❌ Training costs  
❌ May increase short-term costs

### Quality Standards

#### ISO 9001

**International quality management standard**

**Shows**:
- Consistent processes
- Customer focus
- Continuous improvement

**Benefits**:
- Customer confidence
- Improved efficiency
- Opens international markets

## 6.5 Inventory (Stock) Management

### Types of Inventory

1. **Raw Materials**: Materials needed for production
2. **Work-in-Progress (WIP)**: Partly finished products
3. **Finished Goods**: Completed products ready for sale

### Why Hold Inventory?

✓ Meet customer demand immediately  
✓ Cope with unexpected demand increases  
✓ Continue production if supplier delays  
✓ Bulk buying discounts  
✓ Protection against price increases

### Costs of Holding Inventory

❌ **Storage costs**: Warehouse rent, heating, lighting  
❌ **Opportunity cost**: Money tied up in stock  
❌ **Insurance**: Protect against theft/damage  
❌ **Deterioration**: Spoilage, obsolescence  
❌ **Security**: Prevent theft  
❌ **Administration**: Stock management systems

### Stock Control Diagrams

```
Stock Level
↑
|     ┌──────┐   ┌──────┐
|    /│      │  /│      │  Maximum Level
|   / │      │ / │      │
|  /  │      │/  │      │
| /   │      ╱   │      │  Reorder Level
|/    │     /│   │     /│
│     │    / │   │    / │
│     │   /  │   │   /  │  Minimum/Buffer Level
│     │  /   │   │  /   │
└─────┴─┴────┴───┴─┴────┴──→ Time
      ↑      ↑   ↑
   Reorder Delivery Reorder
```

**Key Levels**:
- **Maximum Level**: Highest stock should reach
- **Reorder Level**: Trigger point to order new stock
- **Lead Time**: Time between order and delivery
- **Minimum/Buffer Stock**: Safety level to prevent stockouts

### Just-in-Time (JIT)

**Definition**: Inventory arrives exactly when needed for production, minimizing stock holding

**Origin**: Developed by Toyota in Japan

**Principles**:
- Produce only what's needed
- Exactly when needed
- Zero or minimal inventory

**Requirements**:
- Reliable suppliers
- Efficient logistics
- Quality suppliers (no defects)
- Accurate forecasting
- Flexible production

**Advantages**:
✓ Lower storage costs  
✓ Less waste (fresher stock)  
✓ Cash flow improvement  
✓ Less space required  
✓ Reduced risk of obsolescence

**Disadvantages**:
❌ No buffer if supplier fails  
❌ Can't meet unexpected demand  
❌ Dependent on reliable suppliers  
❌ Increased ordering costs  
❌ Vulnerable to disruptions

**Example**: Dell computers ordered components only when customers placed orders, reducing inventory costs.

## 6.6 Supply Chain Management

### What is a Supply Chain?

**Supply chain** = The network of businesses involved in creating and delivering a product

**Typical Chain**:
```
Raw Material Supplier → Manufacturer → Wholesaler → Retailer → Customer
```

### Supply Chain Objectives

✓ Deliver products on time  
✓ Minimize costs  
✓ Maintain quality  
✓ Manage risks  
✓ Provide value  
✓ Ensure reliability

### Supplier Relationships

#### Choosing Suppliers

**Factors to consider**:
- **Price**: Cost of supplies
- **Quality**: Reliability and standards
- **Reliability**: On-time delivery
- **Capacity**: Can they meet demand?
- **Location**: Transport costs and time
- **Payment terms**: Credit periods offered
- **Ethics**: Sustainable and fair practices

#### Single vs Multiple Suppliers

**Single Supplier**:

**Advantages**:
✓ Bulk discounts  
✓ Strong relationship  
✓ Easier communication  
✓ Consistent quality

**Disadvantages**:
❌ Dependence risk  
❌ Vulnerable to disruption  
❌ Less negotiating power

**Multiple Suppliers**:

**Advantages**:
✓ Competition keeps prices low  
✓ Reduced risk  
✓ Can meet higher demand  
✓ Backup if one fails

**Disadvantages**:
❌ Less negotiating power  
❌ Inconsistent quality  
❌ Complex management  
❌ Higher administration costs

### Logistics

**Definition**: Managing movement and storage of goods

**Includes**:
- Transportation
- Warehousing
- Packaging
- Order processing
- Inventory management

**Objectives**:
- Right product
- Right place
- Right time
- Right condition
- Right cost

## 6.7 Customer Service

### What is Customer Service?

**Customer service** = Support and assistance provided to customers before, during, and after purchase

### Why Customer Service Matters

✓ Increases customer satisfaction  
✓ Builds loyalty and repeat business  
✓ Positive word-of-mouth  
✓ Differentiates from competitors  
✓ Justifies premium pricing  
✓ Reduces complaints

### Elements of Good Customer Service

**Pre-Purchase**:
- Knowledgeable staff
- Product information
- Helpful advice
- Pleasant environment

**During Purchase**:
- Fast service
- Easy payment
- Accurate transactions
- Polite staff

**After-Purchase**:
- Delivery on time
- Easy returns
- Problem resolution
- Warranties
- Feedback requests

### Measuring Customer Service

**Methods**:
1. **Customer surveys**: Feedback questionnaires
2. **Mystery shoppers**: Undercover evaluation
3. **Complaint tracking**: Monitor issues
4. **Net Promoter Score (NPS)**: Likelihood to recommend
5. **Online reviews**: Ratings and comments
6. **Repeat purchase rate**: Customer retention

### Improving Customer Service

**Strategies**:
- Train staff thoroughly
- Empower employees to solve problems
- Respond quickly to complaints
- Personalize service
- Exceed expectations
- Use technology (CRM systems, chatbots)
- Act on feedback

## Key Terminology

- **Operations Management**: Managing production and delivery of goods/services
- **Job Production**: One-off unique products
- **Batch Production**: Groups of identical products
- **Flow Production**: Continuous mass production
- **Cell Production**: Teams completing sections
- **Automation**: Using technology to perform tasks
- **Quality Control**: Checking products for defects
- **Quality Assurance**: Building quality into processes
- **TQM**: Total Quality Management - continuous improvement involving all
- **Inventory**: Stock held by business
- **JIT**: Just-in-Time - minimal stock holding
- **Supply Chain**: Network from raw materials to customer
- **Logistics**: Movement and storage of goods
- **Customer Service**: Support provided to customers
- **Buffer Stock**: Minimum safety stock level
- **Lead Time**: Time between ordering and receiving stock

### Case Study Question

**Case Study: FreshBake Bakery**

FreshBake is a small bakery using batch production to make different types of bread daily. The business is considering three options:

**Option A**: Invest £50,000 in automated dough mixers and ovens  
**Option B**: Implement JIT inventory system for ingredients  
**Option C**: Introduce Total Quality Management (TQM)

Current issues:
- High waste (unsold bread)
- Inconsistent quality
- Customer complaints increasing
- Rising ingredient costs (long storage times)

**Questions**:

a) State the production method currently used by FreshBake. (1 mark)

b) Explain one advantage of batch production for FreshBake. (3 marks)

c) Analyze the benefits and drawbacks of Option A (automation) for FreshBake. (6 marks)

d) Evaluate which option (A, B, or C) would be most beneficial for FreshBake. Justify your recommendation. (12 marks)
